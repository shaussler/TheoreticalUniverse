3D Euclidean space#

by Stéphane Haussler

In this article, I use the language of differential forms to systematically express the derivative, the differential, the gradient, the divergence, the curl and the laplacian in 3-dimensional Euclidean space.

I assume the reader possesses a strong grasp of vector calculus, a working understanding of differential forms, the exterior product, musical operators, as well as the Hodge dual. To learn about differential forms, see yet another great video serie by Michael Penn. Implicitly assumed with the above requisites is a basic understanding of tensor calculus, and in particular the concept of vector and covector. Some familiarity with the concepts of of either Grassman Algebra, Clifford Algebra (AKA Geometric Algebra), or Lie Algebra is not necessary but certainly welcome for a deeper understanding of the antisymmetries at hand.

Derivative#

The partial derivatives are the basis vectors. I am personally still wrapping my head around it and refer for details to Tangent Space Basis and Constructing Tangent Vectors by qncubed3, or Coordinate Basis by brightsideofmaths.

Proposition

\[\mathbf{e}_i = \partial_i\]

Differential#

The differential applied to the partial derivative results in:

\[dx^i ∂_j = δ^i_j\]

This is the definition for a covector, and we conclude that the differential operator is a covector.

Proposition

\[\mathbf{e}^i = dx^i\]

Gradient#

Proposition

\[(df)^{♯} = \mathbf{∇} f\]

Expand \(df\):

\[(df)^♯ = ( dx ∂_x f + dy ∂_y f + dz ∂_z f)^♯\]

Distribute the sharp operator \(♯\):

\[(df)^♯ = (dx)^♯ ∂_x f + (dy)^♯ ∂_y f + (dz)^♯ ∂_z f\]

Which permits to conclude:

\[(df)^♯ = ∂_x f ∂_x + ∂_y f ∂_y + ∂_z f ∂_z\]

In other words and for clarity:

\[(df)^♯ = ∂_x f \mathbf{e}_x + ∂_y f \mathbf{e}_y + ∂_z f \mathbf{e}_z\]

Divergence#

Proposition

\[⋆ d ⋆ F^♭ = \mathbf{∇} \cdot \mathbf{F}\]

We begin with \(F\) as a vector \(\mathbf{F} = F^\sharp = F^i \partial_i\) and flatten:

\[\begin{split}F^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} F^x ∂_x \\ F^y ∂_y \\ F^z ∂_z \\ \end{bmatrix}^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} F^x (∂_x)^♭ \\ F^y (∂_y)^♭ \\ F^z (∂_z)^♭ \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} F^x dx \\ F^y dy \\ F^z dz \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Apply the \(\star\) operator:

\[\begin{split}⋆ F^♭ = ⋆ \begin{bmatrix} F^x dx \\ F^y dy \\ F^z dz \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} F^x ⋆ dx \\ F^y ⋆ dy \\ F^z ⋆ dz \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} F^x dy ∧ dz \\ F^y dz ∧ dx \\ F^z dx ∧ dy \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Apply the \(d\) operator:

\[\begin{split}d ⋆ F^♭ = d \begin{bmatrix} F^x dy ∧ dz \\ F^y dz ∧ dx \\ F^z dx ∧ dy \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ∂_x F^x dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\ ∂_y F^y dy ∧ dz ∧ dx \\ ∂_z F^z dz ∧ dx ∧ dy \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ∂_x F^x dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\ ∂_y F^y dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\ ∂_z F^z dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Which can be brought back to a zero form by applying yet again the Hodge star \(⋆\):

\[\begin{split}⋆ d ⋆ F^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} ∂_x F^x ⋆ dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\ ∂_y F^y ⋆ dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\ ∂_z F^z ⋆ dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ∂_x F^x \mathbf{1} \\ ∂_y F^y \mathbf{1} \\ ∂_z F^z \mathbf{1} \\ \end{bmatrix} = ∂_x F^x + ∂_y F^y + ∂_z F^z\end{split}\]

Curl#

Proposition

\[(⋆(dF^♭))^♯ = ∇^♯ ⨯ F^♯\]

The vector field is:

\[\begin{split}F^♯ = \begin{bmatrix} F^x ∂_x \\ F^y ∂_y \\ F^z ∂_z \\ \end{bmatrix} = F^x ∂_x + F^y ∂_y + F^z ∂_z\end{split}\]

Flattening the vector field result in:

\[\begin{split}F^\flat = \begin{bmatrix} F^x \; dx \\ F^y \; dy \\ F^z \; dz \\ \end{bmatrix} = F^x dx + F^y dy + F^z dz\end{split}\]

Taking the differential, we have:

\[\begin{split}dF^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} ∂_x F^x dx ∧ dx & ∂_y F^x dy ∧ dx & ∂_z F^x dz ∧ dx \\ ∂_x F^y dx ∧ dy & ∂_y F^y dy ∧ dy & ∂_z F^y dz ∧ dy \\ ∂_x F^z dx ∧ dz & ∂_y F^z dy ∧ dy & ∂_z F^z dz ∧ dz \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Or with more natural row/column convention:

\[\begin{split}dF^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} ∂_x F^x dx ∧ dx & ∂_x F^y dx ∧ dy & ∂_x F^z dx ∧ dz \\ ∂_y F^x dy ∧ dx & ∂_y F^y dy ∧ dy & ∂_y F^z dy ∧ dy \\ ∂_z F^x dz ∧ dx & ∂_z F^y dz ∧ dy & ∂_z F^z dz ∧ dz \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Where \(dx^i ∧ dx^i = 0\):

\[\begin{split}dF^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} & ∂_x F^y dx ∧ dy & ∂_x F^z dx ∧ dz \\ ∂_y F^x dy ∧ dx & & ∂_y F^z dy ∧ dy \\ ∂_z F^x dz ∧ dx & ∂_z F^y dz ∧ dy & \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

And \(dx^i ∧ dx^j = -dx^j ∧ dx^i\):

\[\begin{split}dF^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} & + ∂_x F^y dx ∧ dy & - ∂_x F^z dz ∧ dx \\ - ∂_y F^x dx ∧ dy & & + ∂_y F^z dy ∧ dy \\ + ∂_z F^x dz ∧ dx & - ∂_z F^y dy ∧ dz & \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

That we reorder to:

\[\begin{split}dF^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} + ∂_y F^z dy ∧ dy - ∂_z F^y \; dy ∧ dz \\ + ∂_z F^x dz ∧ dx - ∂_x F^z \; dz ∧ dx \\ + ∂_x F^y dx ∧ dy - ∂_y F^x \; dx ∧ dy \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}dF^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} (∂_y F^z - ∂_z F^y) \; dy ∧ dz \\ (∂_z F^x - ∂_x F^z) \; dz ∧ dx \\ (∂_x F^y - ∂_y F^x) \; dx ∧ dy \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Where we can now take the star operator:

\[\begin{split}⋆ dF^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} (∂_y F^z - ∂_z F^y) ⋆ dy ∧ dz \\ (∂_z F^x - ∂_x F^z) ⋆ dz ∧ dx \\ (∂_x F^y - ∂_y F^x) ⋆ dx ∧ dy \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}⋆ dF^♭ = \begin{bmatrix} (∂_y F^z - ∂_z F^y) \; dx \\ (∂_z F^x - ∂_x F^z) \; dy \\ (∂_x F^y - ∂_y F^x) \; dz \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

We can then sharpen the covector to its vector form:

\[\begin{split}(⋆ dF^♭)^♯ = \begin{bmatrix} (∂_y F^z - ∂_z F^y) \; dx^♯ \\ (∂_z F^x - ∂_x F^z) \; dy^♯ \\ (∂_x F^y - ∂_y F^x) \; dz^♯ \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}(⋆ dF^♭)^♯ = \begin{bmatrix} (∂_y F^z - ∂_z F^y) \; ∂_x \\ (∂_z F^x - ∂_x F^z) \; ∂_y \\ (∂_x F^y - ∂_y F^x) \; ∂_t \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Where we have recovered the expression of the curl of a vector field:

\[\begin{split}∇^♯ ⨯ F^♯ = \begin{bmatrix} (∂_y F^z - ∂ F^y) \; ∂_x \\ (∂_z F^x - ∂ F^z) \; ∂_y \\ (∂_x F^y - ∂ F^x) \; ∂_z \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Laplacian#

Proposition

\[⋆ d ⋆ d f = \mathbf{∇}^2 f\]

The differential of a function (zero form) is:

\[df = ∂_x f dx + ∂_y f dy + ∂_z f dz\]

Taking the Hodge dual:

\[⋆ df = ∂_x f dy ∧ dz + ∂_y dz ∧ dx + ∂_z f dx ∧ dy\]

Taking the differential

\[\begin{split}d ⋆ df &= \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ x^2} dx ∧ dy ∧ dz + \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ y^2} dy ∧ dz ∧ dx + \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ z^2} dz ∧ dx ∧ dy \\ &= \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ x^2} dx ∧ dy ∧ dz + \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ y^2} dx ∧ dy ∧ dz + \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ z^2} dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\ &= \left( \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ x^2} + \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ y^2} + \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ z^2} \right) \; dx ∧ dy ∧ dz \\\end{split}\]

Taking the Hodge dual, we tranform volumes to functions and obtain the expression for the Laplacian:

\[⋆ d ⋆ df = \left( \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ x^2} + \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ y^2} + \frac{∂^2 f}{∂ z^2} \right)\]

The Laplacian in vector calculus is the divergence \(\mathbf{∇} \cdot\) applied to the gradient of a function \(\mathbf{∇} f\):

\[\mathbf{∇} \cdot \mathbf{∇} f\]

Where the divergence is \(⋆ d ⋆\) and the gradient of a function is \(df\) brings the zero-form to a zero form.

Laplace-De Rham Operator#

Warning

Draft

The Laplacian is only valid for functions (zero-forms). The Laplacian can be generalized to n-forms with the Laplace-de Rham operator.

\[∆ = dδ + δd = (d + δ)^2\]

Considering a k-form in a space with dimension \(n\) and parity s, the general expression for codifferential is:

\[δ = (-1)^{n(k+1)+1} s ⋆ d ⋆\]

In Euclidean space with \(n=3\), the metric signature is \((+,+,+)\) and the parity therefore \((1)⨯(1)⨯(1)=1\). The codifferential is then:

\[\delta = (-1)^k ⋆ d ⋆\]

Laplace-De Rham Operator in Euclidean Space

\[dδ + δd = d ⋆ d ⋆ + (-1)^k ⋆ d ⋆ d\]

In Minkowski space we have 4 dimensions \(n=4\), the metric signature is \((+,-,-,-)\), and the parity \((+1)⨯(-1)⨯(-1)⨯(-1)=-1\). The codifferential is then:

\[\begin{split}δ & = (-1)^{n(k+1)+1} s ⋆ d ⋆ \\ & = (-1)^{4(k+1)+1} (-1) ⋆ d ⋆ \\ & = (-1)^{4(k+1)+2} ⋆ d ⋆ \\ & = ⋆ d ⋆ \\\end{split}\]

Laplace-De Rham

\[dδ + δd = d ⋆ d ⋆ + ⋆ d ⋆ d\]
