Deriving the Faraday 2‚Äìform from the 1865 Maxwell‚Äôs equations#

The connection to rotations

by St√©phane Haussler

This page presents the geometric differential formulation of electromagnetism, emphasizing the direct connection between Maxwell‚Äôs equations and rotations expressed with differential form. By calculating the exterior derivative these rotations in spacetime and straightforward identification, we will see that the equations governing electromagnetism are, in fact, spacetime rotations with the exterior derivative applied. In layman‚Äôs terms, we can think of electromagnetism as a torque over all possible 6 planes of rotations in 4‚Äìdimensional spacetime.

I hope my approach will feel more intuitive than the conventional method where the electromagnetic 2-form as well as the Maxwell‚Äôs equations in geometric differential form are given upfront, and equivalence to the vector formulation proven backward. For an example of the standard proof, see a nonetheless great video by Michael Penn. The mathematical expressions obtained in this section correspond to the well-recognized differential geometric formulation of Maxwell‚Äôs equations, where \(\mathbf{F}\) is the field 2‚Äìform and \(\mathbf{J}\) the current 3‚Äìform.

\[\begin{split}d\:\mathbf{F} = 0 \\ d‚ãÜ \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{J}\end{split}\]

Unless explicitly stated otherwise, I explicitely indicate the nature of the mathematical objects involved using musical notation. The electromagnetic field 2‚Äìform \(\mathbf{F}\) is written with a double musical flat \(F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}\), the current 3‚Äìform with a triple musical flat \(J^{‚ô≠‚ô≠‚ô≠}\), and the Hodge dual current 1‚Äìform with a single musical flat \(J^‚ô≠\).

This argumentation in this page proceeds as follows: First, I reordered Maxwell‚Äôs equations to align with their original formulation, using modern notation. This reorered form will serve the foundation for expressing the electromagnetic field equations in terms of geometric differential forms. Next, I represent a generic rotation in spacetime to which I apply the exterior derivative. Finally, I identified Maxwell‚Äôs equations with the exterior derivative of this spacetime rotation.

The discussion assumes a solid understanding of the exterior derivative \(d\), √âlie Caretan‚Äôs differential forms, as well as Hodge duality. This page is self-contained and can be read independently of other content on this site.

Vector formulation of Mr. Heaviside#

Mr. Maxwell‚Äôs groundbreaking work in 1865, A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field (pdf), utilized differential expressions rather than the modern vector formulation proposed by Mr. Heaviside. Indeed in 1865, the concept of vectors had not yet been introduced.

Mr. Heaviside proposed both the concept of vectors as well as the vector form of the Maxwell equations. This is the most widespread formulation today. I therefore start from there and unpack into a form closer in spirit to the 1865 equation. The equations consist of two inhomogeneous (Gauss‚Äôs law and Amp√®re‚Äôcircuital law) and two homogeneous vector equations (Gauss‚Äôs law for magnetism and Faraday‚Äôs law of induction), which is how I organize the equations.

While these equations are undoubtedly intriguing, within the context of this derivation, a deep comprehension of the physics behind them is unnecessary. I simply present them as the initial basis for the derivation. With the exception of Maxwell‚Äôs modification to Amp√®re‚Äôs circuital law, these equations represent the mathematical expression of empirical observations. Therefore, they can be regarded as established experimental facts.

Gauss‚Äôs Law

\[\overrightarrow{‚àá} \cdot \overrightarrow{E} = œÅ / Œµ_0\]

Amp√®re‚Äôs Circuital Law

\[\overrightarrow{‚àá} \times \overrightarrow{B} = Œº_0 \overrightarrow{J} + \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇt} \overrightarrow{E}\]

Faraday‚Äôs law of induction

\[\overrightarrow{‚àá} ‚®Ø \overrightarrow{E} = -\frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇt} \overrightarrow{B}\]

Gauss‚Äôs Law for Magnetism

\[\overrightarrow{‚àá} \cdot \overrightarrow{B} = 0\]

With the electric field \(\overrightarrow{E} = \begin{bmatrix} E^x \\ E^y \\ E^z \end{bmatrix}\), magnetic field \(\overrightarrow{B} = \begin{bmatrix} B^x \\ B^y \\ B^z \end{bmatrix}\), and operator \(\overrightarrow{‚àá} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇx} \\ \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇy} \\ \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇz} \end{bmatrix}\)

The equations of Mr. Maxwell#

Unpacking the vector equations into their component form, we obtain in spirit the 1865 Maxwell formulation, albeit with modern notation and conventions.

Gauss‚Äôs Law

\[\frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇx} E^x + \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇy} E^y + \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇz} E^z = œÅ / Œµ_0\]

\[\begin{split}\frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇy} B^z - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇz} B^y = Œº_0 J^x + \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇt} E^x \\ \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇz} B^x - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇx} B^z = Œº_0 J^y + \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇt} E^y \\ \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇx} B^y - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇy} B^x = Œº_0 J^z + \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇt} E^z \\\end{split}\]

Gauss‚Äôs Law for Magnetism

\[\frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇx} B^x + \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇy} B^y + \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇz} B^z = 0\]

Faraday‚Äôs Law of Induction

\[\begin{split}\frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇy} E^z - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇz} E^y = - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇt} B^x \\ \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇz} E^x - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇx} E^z = - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇt} B^y \\ \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇx} E^y - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇy} E^x = - \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇt} B^z \\\end{split}\]

Underlying structure#

Gathering and reordering the terms, a clear structures becomes apparent:

\[\begin{split}\begin{alignedat}{4} & + \frac{‚àÇB^x}{‚àÇx} & + \frac{‚àÇB^y}{‚àÇy} & + \frac{‚àÇB^z}{‚àÇz} &= 0 \\ + \frac{‚àÇB^x}{‚àÇt} & & + \frac{‚àÇE^z}{y‚àÇ} & - \frac{‚àÇE^y}{‚àÇz} &= 0 \\ + \frac{‚àÇB^y}{‚àÇt} & - \frac{‚àÇE^z}{‚àÇx} & & + \frac{‚àÇE^x}{‚àÇz} &= 0 \\ + \frac{‚àÇB^z}{‚àÇt} & + \frac{‚àÇE^y}{‚àÇx} & - \frac{‚àÇE^x}{‚àÇy} & &= 0 \\ \end{alignedat}\end{split}\]

Ordered equations#

Recognizing the emerging structure, we slightly modify the expressions. These modifications are not intricate. The objective is merely to present a compact and symmetrical form, where all terms are aligned.

To eliminate the factor \(1/c\), we introduce \(\tilde{E}^x = E^x / c\), \(\tilde{E}^y = E^y / c\), and \(\tilde{E}^z = E^z / c\). Additionally, we define for the time dimension \(‚àÇ_t = \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇ(ct)}\), and for the spatial dimensions \(‚àÇ_x = \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇ x}\), \(‚àÇ_y = \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇy}\), as well as \(‚àÇ_z = \frac{‚àÇ}{‚àÇz}\). The equations are now:

Inhomogenous equations

\[\begin{split}\begin{alignedat}{4} & + ‚àÇ_x \E^x & + ‚àÇ_y \E^y & + ‚àÇ_z \E^z & = + Œº_0 c œÅ \\ + ‚àÇ_t \E^x & & - ‚àÇ_y B^z & + ‚àÇ_z B^y & = - Œº_0 J^x \\ + ‚àÇ_t \E^y & + ‚àÇ_x B^z & & - ‚àÇ_z B^x & = - Œº_0 J^y \\ + ‚àÇ_t \E^z & - ‚àÇ_x B^y & + ‚àÇ_y B^x & & = - Œº_0 J^z \\ \end{alignedat}\end{split}\]

Homogenous equations

\[\begin{split}\begin{alignedat}{4} & + ‚àÇ_x B^x & + ‚àÇ_y B^y & + ‚àÇ_z B^z & = 0 \\ + ‚àÇ_t B^x & & + ‚àÇ_y \E^z & - ‚àÇ_z \E^y & = 0 \\ + ‚àÇ_t B^y & - ‚àÇ_x \E^z & & + ‚àÇ_z \E^x & = 0 \\ + ‚àÇ_t B^z & + ‚àÇ_x \E^y & - ‚àÇ_y \E^x & & = 0 \\ \end{alignedat}\end{split}\]

For readers well-versed in the tensor formulation of electromagnetism, the presence and nature of the Faraday tensor and its dual are likely evident. Moreover, for those acquainted with matrix multiplication principles, it should be apparent that we can employ matrices operations.

Rotations in differential forms#

General rotations can be expressed as linear combinations of rotations in each planes. For Minkowski space with 4 directions \(‚àÇ_t\), \(‚àÇ_x\), \(‚àÇ_y\) and \(‚àÇ_t\), this result in 6 planes of rotations. Each plance is expressed as the exterior product \(‚àß\) of basis vectors as \(‚àÇ_Œº ‚àß ‚àÇ_ŒΩ\). Rotations in spacetime are expresssed as linear combination of basis rotations in the six available planes of rotations.

Rotations in Minkowski space

\[\begin{split}R^{‚ôØ‚ôØ} = \begin{bmatrix} Q^x \; ‚àÇ_t ‚àß ‚àÇ_x \\ Q^y \; ‚àÇ_t ‚àß ‚àÇ_y \\ Q^z \; ‚àÇ_t ‚àß ‚àÇ_z \\ R^x \; ‚àÇ_y ‚àß ‚àÇ_z \\ R^y \; ‚àÇ_z ‚àß ‚àÇ_x \\ R^z \; ‚àÇ_x ‚àß ‚àÇ_y \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Using the Minkowski metric \(Œ∑\), we flatten a basis vector with the flat operator \(‚ô≠\):

\[(‚àÇ_Œº)^‚ô≠ = Œ∑_{ŒºŒΩ} dx^ŒΩ\]

Likewise any index of the doubly contravariant exterior product can be flattened:

\[\begin{split}\begin{matrix} (‚àÇ_Œº ‚àß ‚àÇ_ŒΩ)^{‚ô≠‚ôØ} &= Œ∑_{Œ≥Œº} dx^Œ≥ ‚àß ‚àÇ_ŒΩ \\ (‚àÇ_Œº ‚àß ‚àÇ_ŒΩ)^{‚ôØ‚ô≠} &= Œ∑_{Œ≥ŒΩ} ‚àÇ_Œº ‚àß dx^Œ≥ \\ (‚àÇ_Œº ‚àß ‚àÇ_ŒΩ)^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} &= Œ∑_{Œ¥Œº} Œ∑_{Œ≥ŒΩ} dx^Œ¥ ‚àß dx^Œ≥ \\ \end{matrix}\end{split}\]

To obtain the doubly covariant representation of rotations in spacetime, we apply the flat operators \(‚ô≠‚ô≠\) to each components \(R^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = (R^{‚ôØ‚ôØ})^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}\):

Doubly covariant representation of rotations in Minkowski space

\[\begin{split}R^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{aligned} - & Q^x \; dt ‚àß dx \\ - & Q^y \; dt ‚àß dy \\ - & Q^z \; dt ‚àß dz \\ & R^x \; dy ‚àß dz \\ & R^y \; dz ‚àß dx \\ & R^z \; dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{aligned} \right]\end{split}\]

The exterior derivative of rotations#

In the article Differential operators expressed as exterior derivatives, I systematically compute the exterior derivative of all possible k‚Äìforms in both 3D Euclidean space and 4D spacetime with the Minkowski metric. The motivation for these systematic calculations is that all differential operators acting on fields (such as the gradient and the laplacian) or vector fields (such as the divergence and the curl) can be expressed using combinations of the Hodge star operator \(‚ãÜ\) and the exterior derivative \(d\).

Since the combinations of these two operators are finite, this approach allows for the calculation of all conceivable differential operators, which are generalized within the framework of exterior calculus. Specifically, we derive expressions for the exterior derivative of rotations and the exterior derivative of their Hodge duals the following:

\[\begin{split}‚ãÜ R^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{aligned} & Q^x \; dy ‚àß dz \\ & Q^y \; dz ‚àß dx \\ & Q^z \; dx ‚àß dy \\ & R^x \; dt ‚àß dx \\ & R^y \; dt ‚àß dy \\ & R^z \; dt ‚àß dz \\ \end{aligned} \right]\end{split}\]

\[\begin{split}d‚ãÜR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} ‚àÇ_t (+ Q^x )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz & + & ‚àÇ_x (+ Q^x )(+1) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ Q^y )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (+ Q^y )(+1) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ Q^z )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ Q^z )(+1) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_y (+ R^x )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ R^x )(-1) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ ‚àÇ_x (+ R^y )(-1) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ R^y )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_x (+ R^z )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (+ R^z )(-1) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Apply values

\[\begin{split}d(‚ãÜR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}) = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} ‚àÇ_t (+ Q^x ) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz & + & ‚àÇ_x (+ Q^x ) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ Q^y ) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (+ Q^y ) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ Q^z ) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ Q^z ) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_y (+ R^x ) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (- R^x ) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ ‚àÇ_x (- R^y ) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ R^y ) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_x (+ R^z ) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (- R^z ) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Rearange

\[\begin{split}d ‚ãÜ R^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & + ‚àÇ_x Q^x & + ‚àÇ_y Q^y & + ‚àÇ_z Q^z & \: ) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t Q^x & & - ‚àÇ_y R^z & + ‚àÇ_z R^y & \: ) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t Q^y & + ‚àÇ_x R^z & & - ‚àÇ_z R^x & \: ) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t Q^z & - ‚àÇ_x R^y & + ‚àÇ_y R^x & & \: ) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Exterior derivative of rotations

\[\begin{split}dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & + ‚àÇ_x R^x & + ‚àÇ_y R^y & + ‚àÇ_z R^z &\:) \; dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^x & & + ‚àÇ_y Q^z & - ‚àÇ_z Q^y &\:) \; dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^y & - ‚àÇ_x Q^z & & + ‚àÇ_z Q^x &\:) \; dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^z & + ‚àÇ_x Q^y & - ‚àÇ_y Q^x & &\:) \; dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Calculations

Distribute the exterior derivative

\[\begin{split}dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \begin{bmatrix} d( - Q^x \; dt ‚àß dx ) \\ d( - Q^y \; dt ‚àß dy ) \\ d( - Q^z \; dt ‚àß dz ) \\ d( + R^x \; dy ‚àß dz ) \\ d( + R^y \; dz ‚àß dx ) \\ d( + R^z \; dx ‚àß dy ) \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Apply the exterior derivative

\[\begin{split}dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{3} ‚àÇ_y (- Q^x ) \; & dy ‚àß dt ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_z (- Q^x ) \; & dz ‚àß dt ‚àß dx \\ ‚àÇ_x (- Q^y ) \; & dx ‚àß dt ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (- Q^y ) \; & dz ‚àß dt ‚àß dy \\ ‚àÇ_x (- Q^z ) \; & dx ‚àß dt ‚àß dz & + & ‚àÇ_y (- Q^z ) \; & dy ‚àß dt ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^x ) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz & + & ‚àÇ_x (+ R^x ) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^y ) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (+ R^y ) \; & dy ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^z ) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ R^z ) \; & dz ‚àß dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Reorder the exterior products

\[\begin{split}dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{3} ‚àÇ_y (- Q^x )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (- Q^x )(-1) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ ‚àÇ_x (- Q^y )(-1) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (- Q^y )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_x (- Q^z )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (- Q^z )(-1) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^x )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz & + & ‚àÇ_x (+ R^x )(+1) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^y )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (+ R^y )(+1) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^z )(+1) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ R^z )(+1) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Simplify

\[\begin{split}dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{3} ‚àÇ_y (- Q^x ) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ Q^x ) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ ‚àÇ_x (+ Q^y ) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (- Q^y ) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_x (- Q^z ) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (+ Q^z ) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^x ) \; & dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz & + & ‚àÇ_x (+ R^x ) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^y ) \; & dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx & + & ‚àÇ_y (+ R^y ) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ ‚àÇ_t (+ R^z ) \; & dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy & + & ‚àÇ_z (+ R^z ) \; & dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Rearange

\[\begin{split}dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & + ‚àÇ_x R^x & + ‚àÇ_y R^y & + ‚àÇ_z R^z &\:) \; dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^x & & + ‚àÇ_y Q^z & - ‚àÇ_z Q^y &\:) \; dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^y & - ‚àÇ_x Q^z & & + ‚àÇ_z Q^x &\:) \; dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^z & + ‚àÇ_x Q^y & - ‚àÇ_y Q^x & &\:) \; dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Hodge dual of the exterior derivative of rotations

\[\begin{split}‚ãÜ dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & - ‚àÇ_x R^x & - ‚àÇ_y R^y & - ‚àÇ_z R^z &\:) \; dt \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^x & & - ‚àÇ_y Q^z & + ‚àÇ_z Q^y &\:) \; dx \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^y & + ‚àÇ_x Q^z & & - ‚àÇ_z Q^x &\:) \; dy \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^z & - ‚àÇ_x Q^y & + ‚àÇ_y Q^x & &\:) \; dz \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Calculations

Apply the Hodge star

Apply the Hodge star to \(dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}\):

\[\begin{split}‚ãÜdR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = ‚ãÜ\left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & + ‚àÇ_x R^x & + ‚àÇ_y R^y & + ‚àÇ_z R^z &\:) \; dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^x & & + ‚àÇ_y Q^z & - ‚àÇ_z Q^y &\:) \; dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^y & - ‚àÇ_x Q^z & & + ‚àÇ_z Q^x &\:) \; dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^z & + ‚àÇ_x Q^y & - ‚àÇ_y Q^x & &\:) \; dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Distribute the Hodge star

\[\begin{split}‚ãÜdR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & + ‚àÇ_x R^x & + ‚àÇ_y R^y & + ‚àÇ_z R^z &\:) \; ‚ãÜ dx^x ‚àß dx^y ‚àß dx^z \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^x & & + ‚àÇ_y Q^z & - ‚àÇ_z Q^y &\:) \; ‚ãÜ dx^t ‚àß dx^y ‚àß dx^z \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^y & - ‚àÇ_x Q^z & & + ‚àÇ_z Q^x &\:) \; ‚ãÜ dx^t ‚àß dx^z ‚àß dx^x \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^z & + ‚àÇ_x Q^y & - ‚àÇ_y Q^x & &\:) \; ‚ãÜ dx^t ‚àß dx^x ‚àß dx^y \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Apply the Hodge star to the basis 1-forms

Using the tables for the Hodge dual:

\[\begin{split}‚ãÜdR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & + ‚àÇ_x R^x & + ‚àÇ_y R^y & + ‚àÇ_z R^z &\:) \; (-dt) \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^x & & + ‚àÇ_y Q^z & - ‚àÇ_z Q^y &\:) \; (-dx) \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^y & - ‚àÇ_x Q^z & & + ‚àÇ_z Q^x &\:) \; (-dy) \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t R^z & + ‚àÇ_x Q^y & - ‚àÇ_y Q^x & &\:) \; (-dz) \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Conclude

\[\begin{split}‚ãÜdR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & - ‚àÇ_x R^x & - ‚àÇ_y R^y & - ‚àÇ_z R^z &\:) \; dt \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^x & & - ‚àÇ_y Q^z & + ‚àÇ_z Q^y &\:) \; dx \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^y & + ‚àÇ_x Q^z & & - ‚àÇ_z Q^x &\:) \; dy \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^z & - ‚àÇ_x Q^y & + ‚àÇ_y Q^x & &\:) \; dz \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

Identifying the Faraday 2‚Äìform#

To summarize the results and observations thus far, an arbitrary rotation in Minkowski space is represented by a 2‚Äìform:

By applying the exterior derivative \(d\) to this rotation or to its Hodge dual (effectively calculating a torque), we obtain:

\[\begin{split}d‚ãÜR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & + ‚àÇ_x Q^x & + ‚àÇ_y Q^y & + ‚àÇ_z Q^z &\:) \; dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t Q^x & & - ‚àÇ_y R^z & + ‚àÇ_z R^y &\:) \; dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t Q^y & + ‚àÇ_x R^z & & - ‚àÇ_z R^x &\:) \; dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx \\ (& + ‚àÇ_t Q^z & - ‚àÇ_x R^y & + ‚àÇ_y R^x & &\:) \; dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}‚ãÜ dR^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{alignedat}{5} (& & - ‚àÇ_x R^x & - ‚àÇ_y R^y & - ‚àÇ_z R^z &\:) \; dt \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^x & & - ‚àÇ_y Q^z & + ‚àÇ_z Q^y &\:) \; dx \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^y & + ‚àÇ_x Q^z & & - ‚àÇ_z Q^x &\:) \; dy \\ (& - ‚àÇ_t R^z & - ‚àÇ_x Q^y & + ‚àÇ_y Q^x & &\:) \; dz \\ \end{alignedat} \right]\end{split}\]

We have also reordered the 1865 Maxwell equations to:

Inhomogenous equations

\[\begin{split}\begin{alignedat}{4} & + ‚àÇ_x \E^x & + ‚àÇ_y \E^y & + ‚àÇ_z \E^z & = + Œº_0 c œÅ \\ + ‚àÇ_t \E^x & & - ‚àÇ_y B^z & + ‚àÇ_z B^y & = - Œº_0 J^x \\ + ‚àÇ_t \E^y & + ‚àÇ_x B^z & & - ‚àÇ_z B^x & = - Œº_0 J^y \\ + ‚àÇ_t \E^z & - ‚àÇ_x B^y & + ‚àÇ_y B^x & & = - Œº_0 J^z \\ \end{alignedat}\end{split}\]

Homogenous equations

\[\begin{split}\begin{alignedat}{4} & + ‚àÇ_x B^x & + ‚àÇ_y B^y & + ‚àÇ_z B^z & = 0 \\ + ‚àÇ_t B^x & & + ‚àÇ_y \E^z & - ‚àÇ_z \E^y & = 0 \\ + ‚àÇ_t B^y & - ‚àÇ_x \E^z & & + ‚àÇ_z \E^x & = 0 \\ + ‚àÇ_t B^z & + ‚àÇ_x \E^y & - ‚àÇ_y \E^x & & = 0 \\ \end{alignedat}\end{split}\]

Identification

Identifiying the components of the electric field \(\tilde{E}^i=E^i/c\) and the magnetic field \(B^i\) is straightforward:

\[\begin{split}\begin{matrix} \E^x = R^x \\ \E^y = R^y \\ \E^z = R^z \\ B^x = Q^x \\ B^y = Q^y \\ B^z = Q^z \\ \end{matrix}\end{split}\]

The Faraday 2‚Äìform \(F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}\) is a rotation in Minkowski spacetime.

\[\begin{split}F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = \left[ \begin{aligned} - & \E^x \; dt ‚àß dx \\ - & \E^y \; dt ‚àß dy \\ - & \E^z \; dt ‚àß dz \\ & B^x \; dy ‚àß dz \\ & B^y \; dz ‚àß dx \\ & B^z \; dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{aligned} \right]\end{split}\]

Maxwell‚Äôs equations are obtained by applying the exterior derivative, both directly \(dF^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}\) and to its Hodge dual \(d‚ãÜF^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}\):

Inhomogenous Maxwell equations

\[\begin{split}d\:‚ãÜ \left[ \begin{aligned} - & \E^x \; dt ‚àß dx \\ - & \E^y \; dt ‚àß dy \\ - & \E^z \; dt ‚àß dz \\ & B^x \; dy ‚àß dz \\ & B^y \; dz ‚àß dx \\ & B^z \; dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{aligned} \right] = \begin{bmatrix} + Œº_0 c œÅ \; dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz\\ - Œº_0 J^x \; dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz\\ - Œº_0 J^y \; dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx\\ - Œº_0 J^z \; dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy\\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Homogenous Maxwell equations

\[\begin{split}d \left[ \begin{aligned} - & \E^x \; dt ‚àß dx \\ - & \E^y \; dt ‚àß dy \\ - & \E^z \; dt ‚àß dz \\ & B^x \; dy ‚àß dz \\ & B^y \; dz ‚àß dx \\ & B^z \; dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{aligned} \right] = 0\end{split}\]

We have flipped the sign of the ordered homegenous Maxwell equations to our conveniance to ensure the correct overall sign. With our explicit notation, using a double musical flat ‚ô≠‚ô≠ to identify 2‚Äìforms, and a single musical flat ‚ô≠ to identify 1‚Äìforms, we have:

\[\begin{split}d\:F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} &= 0 \\ d‚ãÜ F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} &= J^‚ô≠ \\\end{split}\]

As advertised in the introduction and using the implicit notation of the wikipedia article, we fall back to the well-recognized differential geometric formulation of Maxwell‚Äôs equations, where \(\mathbf{F}\) is the field 2‚Äìform and \(\mathbf{J}\) is the current 3‚Äìform.

\[\begin{split}d\:\mathbf{F} &= 0 \\ d‚ãÜ \mathbf{F} &= \mathbf{J} \\\end{split}\]

A single equation#

Adding a further Hodge star to the Homogenous equations is equally valid and we can equally write \(d\:F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = 0\) or \(‚ãÜd\:F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}=0\). With our explicit notation using double or single musical flats to idenfity 1‚Äìforms (‚ô≠) and 2‚Äìforms (‚ô≠‚ô≠), and since \(‚ãÜ\:d\:F\) is a 1‚Äìform and \(d‚ãÜF\) a 3‚Äìform, we notice that we can unambiguously merge inhomogeneous and homogeneous equations into a single equation [note2]:

\[\begin{split}d ‚ãÜ \left[ \begin{aligned} - & \E^x \; dt ‚àß dx \\ - & \E^y \; dt ‚àß dy \\ - & \E^z \; dt ‚àß dz \\ & B^x \; dy ‚àß dz \\ & B^y \; dz ‚àß dx \\ & B^z \; dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{aligned} \right] + ‚ãÜ\:d \left[ \begin{aligned} - & \E^x \; dt ‚àß dx \\ - & \E^y \; dt ‚àß dy \\ - & \E^z \; dt ‚àß dz \\ & B^x \; dy ‚àß dz \\ & B^y \; dz ‚àß dx \\ & B^z \; dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{aligned} \right] = \begin{bmatrix} + Œº_0 c œÅ \; dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz\\ - Œº_0 J^x \; dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz\\ - Œº_0 J^y \; dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx\\ - Œº_0 J^z \; dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy\\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Maxwell‚Äôs equations in differential form

\[\begin{split}(d ‚ãÜ + ‚ãÜ d ) \left[ \begin{aligned} - & \E^x \; dt ‚àß dx \\ - & \E^y \; dt ‚àß dy \\ - & \E^z \; dt ‚àß dz \\ & B^x \; dy ‚àß dz \\ & B^y \; dz ‚àß dx \\ & B^z \; dx ‚àß dy \\ \end{aligned} \right] = \left[ \begin{aligned} + Œº_0 c œÅ \; dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz\\ - Œº_0 J^x \; dt ‚àß dy ‚àß dz\\ - Œº_0 J^y \; dt ‚àß dz ‚àß dx\\ - Œº_0 J^z \; dt ‚àß dx ‚àß dy\\ \end{aligned} \right]\end{split}\]

With a shorthand \(F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠}\) for the electromagnetic field 2-form and \(J^{‚ô≠‚ô≠‚ô≠}\) for the current 3-form, we conclude with the compact form [note3]:

Maxwell‚Äôs equations

\[(d ‚ãÜ + ‚ãÜ d) \; F^{‚ô≠‚ô≠} = J^{‚ô≠‚ô≠‚ô≠}\]

Maxwell‚Äôs equations are interpreted as a twist in spacetime, with a strength proportional to the 4-current.

Reducing to a single equation using the wikipedia notation

Using the wikipedia notation, we arrive at the same result. However the lack of an explicit distinction between 1‚Äìforms and 2‚Äìforms is unfortunate as it makes it harder to see the reduction to a single equation.

\[‚ãÜ \: d \: \mathbf{F} + d‚ãÜ\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{J}\]

Since \(d\:\mathbf{F}\) is a 3‚Äìform, its Hodge dual \(‚ãÜ\:d\:\mathbf{F}\) is a 1‚Äìform. We can thus rewrite the homogenous equation as acting on 1‚Äìforms, and the inhomogeneous equations as acting on 3‚Äìforms:

\[\begin{split}\begin{alignedat}{2} ‚ãÜ \: d \: \mathbf{F} &= 0 & \qquad\text{1-forms} \\ d‚ãÜ\mathbf{F} &= \mathbf{J} & \qquad\text{3-forms} \\ \end{alignedat}\end{split}\]

Expanding the right hand side of both equations for the argument, we have:

\[\begin{split}‚ãÜ \: d \: \mathbf{F} =& \: 0\:dt + 0\:dx + 0\:dy + 0\:dz \\ d‚ãÜ\mathbf{F} =& \: \mu_0 œÅ dx‚àßdy‚àßdz - Œº_0 J^x dt‚àßdy‚àßdz - Œº_0 J^y dt‚àßdz‚àßdx - Œº_0 J^z dt‚àßdx‚àßdy \\\end{split}\]

Therefore, the differential geometric formulation can be unambiguously reduced to a single equation involving both 3‚Äìforms and 1‚Äìforms:

\[‚ãÜ \: d \: \mathbf{F} + d‚ãÜ\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{J}\]

Notes#

[note2]

An equation containing 3-forms and 2-forms indeed cannot be reduced. For example, the following equation: \(a \; dx ‚àß dy + b \; dx ‚àß dy ‚àß dz = c \; dx ‚àß dy\) cannot be simplified. Surface 2-forms and volume 3-forms are distinct objects, but they can be represented in the same equation using the \(+\) symbol, even though they cannot actually be added together. Similar examples include combining the real and imaginary parts of complex numbers, or adding bivectors and trivectors in Clifford algebra. With the exemplary equation above, we thus necessarily have \(a = c\) as well as \(b = 0\). This is how we can write the Maxwell equations via differential forms into a single equation.

[note3]

Flipping the sign of \(‚ãÜ d\) is equally valid.
